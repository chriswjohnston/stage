Priority · Communication & Technology

Residents Shouldn't Have to
Dig for Information About
Their Own Government

The Township of Nipissing hasn't published a newsletter since September 2024. By-laws and resolutions aren't searchable. Key documents are buried in PDFs with no plain-language summaries. I didn't wait to be elected to start fixing this. I built the tools myself — and I'm showing what's possible.

The Current State

What Residents Are Working With Today

This isn't a criticism of staff — the Township website is maintained by a small team with no dedicated communications role. It's a criticism of a council that hasn't treated resident communication as a priority. Here's the documented reality.

📰

Newsletter: Last Published September 2024 — 18 Months Ago

The Township's official newsletter — the primary way most residents learn about local government — hasn't been published since September 2024. The newsletter page shows four issues in 2024, then silence. No announcement, no substitute, no explanation to residents.

🔍

By-Laws and Resolutions: Not Searchable, Not Complete

The Township by-laws page lists some documents but doesn't allow keyword, topic, or date search. Resolutions are embedded inside meeting minutes PDFs — to find a specific council decision, a resident must download and read through dozens of documents. There is no way to simply search "what did council decide about the landfill?"

📄

Council Meetings: A List of PDF Links, Nothing More

The council meetings page is a plain list of PDF links. Residents who want to know what happened at the last meeting must download multi-page documents and parse procedural language to find the decisions that affect them. No summaries, no highlights, no email notifications when new documents are posted.

📊

Key Plans: Buried in Large PDFs With No Entry Point

The Asset Management Plan, audited financial statements, strategic plan, and energy consumption plan are each a single link to a large PDF. No summary, no key figures, no plain-language explanation of what any of it means for residents. These documents are produced at taxpayer expense — residents deserve to be able to read them.

By-Law & Resolution Archive

bylaw.chriswjohnston.ca

Every by-law and resolution extracted from meeting minutes and agenda packages — organized by year, searchable by keyword, and enriched with AI-generated plain-language summaries so residents can understand what was decided without reading legal language.

The Township's own by-laws page offers none of this functionality. This archive was built specifically to fill that gap.

Visit the archive →

Local News Aggregator & Email Digest

Pulls from Township, Fire Dept, Recreation, Commanda Centre, Museum & more · Biweekly email digest

A daily automated scraper pulls posts from six local Facebook pages and RSS feeds — the Township of Nipissing, Nipissing Fire Department, Nipissing Recreation, Commanda Community Centre, Nipissing Township Museum, and Commanda General Store Museum — aggregating everything into a single searchable feed.

Residents currently need to follow six separate Facebook pages and check the Township website independently. This consolidates all of it automatically.

A biweekly email digest is sent to subscribers summarizing what's happened across the township — the proactive communication the Township's newsletter used to provide before it went dark in September 2024.

This runs on GitHub Actions at essentially no cost, requires no CMS or dedicated staff, and delivers a better result than the current approach of publishing quarterly PDFs when someone remembers to.

💡

The Point Isn't to Replace the Township — It's to Show What's Possible

These tools prove that better communication is achievable with modest effort and near-zero cost. If a single candidate with no staff can build an automated council archive, a searchable by-law database, and a news digest, a municipality with a budget and real resources can do far more. The barrier isn't technical — it's whether council treats communication as a priority. Right now, it doesn't.

If Elected · My Commitments

Five Specific Things
I Would Advocate For

These aren't vague promises to "improve communication." Each one is specific, achievable, and grounded in work I've already proven is possible.

1

Revive the Newsletter — as a Digital-First Email Digest

A quarterly paper newsletter is slow and expensive. A regular email digest — summarizing council decisions in plain language, upcoming meetings, and community updates — costs nothing to produce and reaches residents the same day. Opt-in, free, and far more useful than a PDF mailed once a quarter.

2

Plain-Language Summaries for Every Major Decision

When council approves a capital project, passes a by-law, or makes a budget decision, a one-paragraph plain-language summary should be posted to the website and included in the digest. Not a resolution number and legal language — a sentence a resident can understand without a law degree.

3

Searchable By-Laws and Resolutions on the Township Website

The Township by-laws page should allow search by keyword, year, or topic — functionality I've already built and proven at bylaw.chriswjohnston.ca. This is a one-time implementation, not an ongoing cost. I would make it a specific deliverable in the Township's communications strategy.

4

Accessible Summaries for Key Plans and Reports

The Asset Management Plan, capital budget, audited financials, and strategic plan should each have a plain-language summary page — not just a PDF download. Residents fund these documents; they deserve to be able to read them without a professional planning background.

5

Consistent Social Media — Not Just at Election Time

A consistent cadence of posts — meeting reminders, decision summaries, community updates — costs nothing but intention. I would advocate for a formal communications policy that sets minimum standards for how and when the Township communicates with residents online.
