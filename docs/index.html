A Pattern Worth Discussing

Three Terms.
Three Mayors. The Same Story.

For three consecutive council terms, Nipissing Township has seen its mayor's seat change hands mid-term by appointment rather than election. Councillors have departed, vacancies have been filled by council vote rather than by residents, and in one case council used a vacancy to return someone voters had just rejected at the ballot box. The institutional knowledge needed to see projects through keeps walking out the door — and nobody asks why.

Five seats were filled by voters in October 2022: Piper (Mayor), Yemm, Foote, Kirkey, and Butler. Butler resigned in 2023 — council appointed James Scott, who had run in 2022 and lost, overriding the public's decision at the ballot box. 🗳️

Council Used a Vacancy to Override What Voters Decided

When Tom Butler resigned, council appointed James Scott — a candidate voters had rejected just months earlier in the October 2022 election. Regardless of Scott's qualifications, the democratic principle is clear: residents chose not to elect him, and council chose to seat him anyway. A vacancy appointment process is supposed to fill an unexpected gap — not to relitigate an election result.

Why Consistency Is the Foundation of Good Governance

Running a township isn't like chairing a one-time committee. It requires understanding multi-year capital plans, the history behind every budget line, relationships with provincial partners, and the context behind hundreds of past decisions. When councillors leave mid-term, all of that walks out with them. This is how a major software implementation gets approved and abandoned without anyone at the table asking why.

📋 Multi-Year Projects Stall

Capital projects, grant applications, and infrastructure plans span multiple years. Every new councillor restarts the learning curve — delaying decisions and increasing the risk of costly mistakes.

🤝 Relationships Take Time

Effective municipal governance depends on relationships with provincial ministries, neighbouring councils, and community organizations. These can't be rebuilt every time someone leaves.

🗳️ Appointments Aren't Votes

When a seat is filled by appointment rather than election, residents lose their voice. The person at the table was chosen by four colleagues, not by the community they're supposed to represent.

⚡ Momentum Is Everything

Progress on facilities, communication, and infrastructure requires sustained attention over years. Churn kills momentum. Consistency gets things done.

The Real Issues

What Needs to Change

These aren't abstract positions. They're patterns I've observed in our township — and problems I'm committed to fixing.

01
⚠ Accountability

Township Software — Approved Without Scrutiny, Abandoned After Three Years

In 2022, council approved a multi-system software package — TownSuite for finance and payroll ($179,733), CGIS for cemetery and building ($3,800), and Firepro for fire and by-law ($5,685) — totalling approximately $189,000 plus taxes. After three years of failed implementation, TownSuite was terminated in late 2025 and replaced with a lower-cost alternative. No public explanation was given for what went wrong, what the final cost to taxpayers was, or what will be done differently next time. I will ask those questions — every time.

02
Stability

Three Terms of Turnover — It's Time for Consistency

For three consecutive terms, the mayor's seat has changed hands mid-term. Council has appointed replacements — including someone voters had just rejected at the ballot box. Institutional knowledge evaporates. Projects stall. I am committed to serving the full term and being present for every decision that matters.

03
Governance

Not Enough Questions at the Council Table

Good governance requires healthy, informed debate. When TownSuite was approved in 2022, the questions that should have been asked include: What does implementation look like? Who is accountable for milestones? What happens if it fails? When it was terminated in 2025 and replaced with a new solution, the questions that should have been asked include: What went wrong? What did this cost in total? What is the new system and why is it the right fit? These aren't complicated questions — but they have to be asked. I will ask them.

04
Skills & Capacity

A Stronger Council Means Complementary Skills

The current council brings real strengths — particularly in roads, infrastructure, and the hands-on operational knowledge that comes from long experience with this township. Those skills matter and deserve respect. What's missing is diversity at the table: marketing, communications, technology, and financial analysis. A well-rounded council doesn't need everyone to know everything — it needs people who collectively cover the range of decisions in front of them. I'm not here to replace existing expertise. I'm here to add what's currently missing.

05
Communication

Modern Communication Tools — Complementing What Already Works

The paper newsletter, the township website, and direct mail for tax bills all serve residents who rely on them — and they should continue. What's missing are modern complements: a digital newsletter for residents who prefer email, easier-to-navigate information on the website so residents can find what they're looking for without calling the office, and optional electronic property tax billing for those who want it. These additions serve new residents and younger families without leaving anyone behind.
