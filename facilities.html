Fiscal Responsibility · Long-Term Planning

Where Is the Vision
for Our Township?

Nipissing Township has a $4.8 million facilities problem on the horizon and no clear plan to fund it. The 2025 Asset Management Plan documents the need. The capital budgets acknowledge it. But there is no community consultation, no design work, no reserve strategy, and no sense of urgency — just a placeholder in a spreadsheet dated 2035.

The Asset Management Plan · 2025

What the Township's Own Documents Say

In October 2025, the Township published its Asset Management Plan, prepared by UrbanRe Advisory. It identifies $4,812,500 in facilities and land improvement needs between 2026 and 2035. The table below is drawn directly from that plan.

The Single Biggest Line Item Has No Reserve and No Plan

The Public Works Garage and Township Office replacement — estimated at $4,000,000 — is the largest single capital expenditure in the township's 10-year forecast. The near-term garage repair ($167,500) has a dedicated reserve being built. The Community Centre ($175,000) has a reserve. But the $4 million new facility? There is no reserve, no design, no community consultation, and no grant strategy. It simply appears on a spreadsheet as a future problem.

Analysis · Capital Budget Critiques

Three Gaps in the Current Approach

Having reviewed the 2024–2034 forecast, the 2025–2035 reconsideration, and the 2026 capital forecast, here are the planning gaps that need to be addressed.

Critique 1 · Reserve Strategy

Reserves for the Small Projects. Nothing for the Big One.

The capital budgets show disciplined reserve planning for smaller recurring assets — garage maintenance ($25,000/yr), the community centre, fire apparatus, bridges. This is good governance. But the $4,000,000 facility replacement that the Township's own Asset Management Plan identifies as a priority? No reserve contribution appears anywhere across the 2025–2035 or 2026–2036 forecasts.

At $25,000 per year — the same rate as the garage maintenance reserve — it would take 160 years to accumulate $4 million. Even at $100,000 per year starting now, the township would have less than $1 million by 2035. Without a reserve strategy, this project will either be deferred indefinitely or funded entirely through debt financing at the worst possible time.

Critique 2 · Designated Broadband Reserve

$60,000 Sitting in a Broadband Reserve — With No Project Attached

The 2025 capital budget reconsideration shows a Designated Broadband Reserve with a balance of $60,000 as of October 2024. No corresponding broadband project appears anywhere in the 2025–2035 or 2026–2036 capital forecasts.

This raises a straightforward question: what is this reserve for? Broadband infrastructure in rural Ontario is primarily funded through federal and provincial programs — not by small municipalities building their own networks. If there is no defined project, no timeline, and no matching grant opportunity attached to this reserve, council should reconsider whether these funds are better directed toward the $4 million facility gap or another identified priority. Reserves without a plan are just deferred decisions.

Critique 3 · Community Vision

A $4 Million Building Decision Made Without the Community

The Asset Management Plan recommends replacing both the Township Office and the Public Works Garage with a single new facility. This is potentially the most significant capital project in Nipissing Township in a generation. It will shape how the township operates, where staff work, and how residents interact with their local government for decades.

Yet there has been no community consultation, no public presentation of options, no visioning exercise, and no design work. The 2026 capital forecast includes a note that the Public Works Garage replacement is "if funding available" — which signals no plan to make funding available. A project of this scale requires years of groundwork: community input, schematic drawings, a reserve fund, and a grant strategy. None of these have been started.

My Proposal

A Three-Step Plan for
Long-Term Facilities

The answer isn't to panic about $4 million — it's to start making deliberate decisions now so we're not scrambling in 2034. Here's what I would advocate for as a councillor.

What I Would Do

Start the Work Now So We're Ready When the Money Comes

  1. 1

    Community Consultations on Long-Term Building Needs

    Before spending a dollar on design, ask residents what they need. What services should be co-located? Is there an opportunity to build a multi-use facility that serves both the Township and the community? What does Nipissing Township's civic anchor look like in 2040? These are questions that should be answered through public consultation — not decided quietly by staff and council. A structured engagement process in 2026–2027 creates legitimacy and community buy-in for whatever comes next.

  2. 2

    Commission Concept Drawings and Designs

    Provincial and federal infrastructure grant programs consistently favour applicants who come prepared — with site assessments, schematic designs, and cost estimates in hand. A township that shows up with a napkin sketch loses to one that shows up with drawings. Investing $30,000–$50,000 in concept-level design work now could unlock millions in grant funding later. This also forces the project to become real: a drawing makes decisions tangible in a way a line item on a spreadsheet never does.

  3. 3

    Establish a Dedicated Reserve Fund

    Start building matching dollars now. Many infrastructure grant programs require a municipal contribution — typically 25–50% of project costs. A $4 million project at 33% municipal share requires $1.3 million in reserve. At $130,000 per year starting in 2027, we'd have that by 2037. It's achievable — but only if we start. I would advocate for a dedicated "Civic Facilities Reserve" as a line item in the 2027 budget, and review the Designated Broadband Reserve to determine whether those funds should be redirected here.

🌾

This Is What Planning Actually Looks Like

On a farm, you don't wait for the barn roof to collapse before you think about replacing it. You look at its condition, you budget for repairs, you set money aside, and when the right opportunity comes — a good price, a grant, a windfall year — you're ready to act. Municipal capital planning works the same way. The township has done the condition assessment. Now it needs to do the planning.
